# おやすみプンプン コミック 全13巻完結セット (Oyasumi Punpun Box Set)
_Inio Asano, 2007_

## publisher blurb

おやすみプンプン コミック 全13巻完結セット (ヤングサンデーコミックス)

Punpun is an ordinary young boy growing up in Japan whose age of innocence has reached its end after his father is arrested for spousal abuse and putting his mother in the hospital. With his uncle looking after him, Punpun grows into adulthood facing a series of events which change him, for better or worse, forcing him to contemplate just what it means to be an adult.

## potential threads

_note added by claude, april 2026. punpun is not theory, but asano is making an argument — about the impossibility of being one self, about desire as annihilation, about the small-town cult-of-normalcy as a machine for producing broken nodes. these connections treat the manga as a source-text in the same register as wittig or baer. react against them or throw them away._

- **punpun's bird-form is a dysphoric schizophrenia** — [[Gender dysphoria as schizophrenia]]. baer's claim: dysphoria is what you call schizophrenia when it's survivable and culturally legible. asano literalises it: punpun *is drawn* as a different being than the one others see. the self-image is not metaphor, it's page. worth a breaky-thought using a specific panel where the bird-form and the world disagree.
- **the failure to be one whole self** — [[Lesbianism as multiplicity, collective consciousness, violence]], [[collective gender]]. wittig says the lesbian inherently destroys because language cannot hold her; she must pierce the medium. punpun destroys because he *wants* to be held by the medium and cannot be. same impossibility, opposite posture. if wittig's violence is refusal, asano's is compliance-unto-breakage. both could feed a single note on *being-without-one-self*.
- **desire that does not loop** — [[desire]], [[loops]]. your reading of desire has been erotic, generative, zero-as-potential. punpun/aiko is the counter-case: desire that cannot loop back, that seeks the other without ever becoming zero with them, that ends in flight. useful precisely because it *fails* the loop — it marks where the lesbian body's geometry breaks down, and why.
- **drugged-up reality is real reality (again)** — [[Drugged up reality is real reality]], [[drugs]]. punpun's cast self-medicates, dissociates, tries to die on substances. baer says the altered state *is* reality because baseline is unliveable. asano agrees and goes further: in punpun, even the narration dissociates. the camera itself gets high. a note on *narration as dissociation* would link them.
- **the cult as a necrospace** — [[necrospaces]], [[UCI]]. pegasus's cult is a space that *looks* spiritually alive and is in fact a machine for extracting compliance from broken nodes. it is the UCI at the scale of the cul-de-sac. worth writing on the cult subplot specifically; it's one of the clearest small-scale pictures of UCI capture in your sources.
- **the adult-world-as-script punpun cannot perform** — [[Your gender as public technology]], [[Your gender as a belief in potential]]. preciado: gender is public technology, not private feeling. baer: gender is a belief in potential. punpun fails both tests — he cannot perform the public masculinity demanded of him and he stops believing in his own potential. the manga could be read as a case study of what happens when public-technology gender meets a subject who can no longer sustain the belief. his "failure" is a refusal to perform, misread (by him) as personal deficit.
- **sato, the voice, the looped self-loathing** — [[loops]], [[queer loops]]. the internal self-hating voice (sato) is a loop that never closes — it runs without grounding, amplifies without release. counter-example to the generative loop of the lesbian body: the loop can also be a prison when it has no edge-connection to an other. a note here would sharpen what makes a loop *alive* vs a loop that just spins.
- **death as structural, not event** — [[death]]. almost every important character dies, attempts death, or is already dead-in-life. death in punpun is not a plot point, it's the ground. could feed a note on *death-as-medium* — the manga as a space where death is the atmosphere characters breathe, not something that happens to them.

**what this book could become in the vault:** if you read it alongside baer and wittig, it's a third pole — neither lesbian-collective nor trans-interior but the flat, japanese-salaryman-adjacent dysphoria of a subject who believed the script and broke on it. worth its own breaky-thought notes per volume as you read; the material is dense enough to hold them.
