*" "Deep down, what is the experience of drugs, if not this: to erase limits, to reject divisions, to put away all prohibitions, and then ask oneself the question, what has become of knowledge? Do we then know something altogether other? Can we still know what we knew before the experience of drugs? Is this knowledge of before drugs still valid, or is it a new knowledge? This is a real problem and I think that in this measure the experience of drugs isn’t original in our society, it’s not a sort of little deviance that does not count. It seems to me that it is at the very heart of problems that the society in which we live—that is to say, in the capitalist society—is confronted with."Michel Foucault, Interview, 1971" p. 23*

Can we still know what we knew before experiencing the drug? Yes, definitely. Is it still valid, though? Yes, it can definitely be. But is it a new knowledge? Also, yes. To many, the answer is no, though. 

The answer stays valid, but your PERCEPTION of reality might have reverted back to a state where the answer was not valid before. It's like seeing something under a microscope for the first time; you first did not know that this specific attribute of a molecule existed, and now you do. Removing the microscope does not affect this; your finding remains true. 

Then why do many not think those answers remain valid? Because they believe drugs MISform your perception of reality, not just make you see things in a new, similarly valid, light. 

What can fix this? Literature like Reality Switch Technologies being taught in schools, probably.

Doing k, hannah understands something that she did not understand before, while sober. Being trans and sober in the world and in the "wrong" body feels like an enormous weight, but ketamine lifts that weight and allows you to sprint, it lifts the heaviness of the world. A trans person's gender can misalign with how they look in other humans' eyes, which is that gigantic weight. K lifts that weight, the dysphoria. But the dysphoria is acually the worlds wrongdoing towards the trans person ...
So it doesnt really do anything to you as a person, it just lifts the veil and allows you to see yourself how u really are, without pain. *"Which is important for being trans, to be able to wach yourself, to feel your body differently." (p. 29)* To see how full of possibility you are when the weight of dysphoria is lifted, when a world exists without it. How zeroed in you are, how full of potential. Those small moments when we're able to lift that veil, be it through disconnect from perception or drug-induced 'mis'perception, that is when we are free. Then, we lose the schizo term, everything is open.
