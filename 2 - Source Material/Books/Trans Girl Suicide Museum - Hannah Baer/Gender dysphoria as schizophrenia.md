*"When I tell cis people that I’ve always wanted to be a girl, I also usually tell them that there’s a lot of privilege in this narrative, because it’s recognized by medical and cultural establishments as the valid trans narrative. [...] "i do feel like i was born in the wrong body" "" p. 25*

Not Queering, always having had one gender / sexuality, is more relatable to the cis-het experience and thus privileged. NB, genderfluid, or people that queer within their transness are much less privileged. Feeling like you were born in the wrong body is "acceptable" to some, they understand it, because you know it is one single type of body you would ave rather wanted. But the "right" body being a different one every day is harder to grasp. Queering is hard to grasp because it changes place, time, form, color, vibe, every moment of every day. In this light, it can be related to a state of schizophrenia.. where nothing is ever correct, everything is always changing. You were born in the wrong body, the wrong society, the wrong understanding of the world, the wrong nature / physicality .. The world is wrong, not you. 





"*there’s this idea in philosophy in the last 60 years that the category of a person (or “a subject”) is actually socially constructed, rather than natural and/ or handed down from g-d. The idea is something like “what if this category of ‘person,’ along with all its supposedly ‘natural’ elements (gender, erotic desire, emotions, intelligence, etc.) is actually produced by culture rather than nature?” I believed this idea—that the ‘natural subject’ was not natural—for a long time but I wasn’t sure what it meant, like practically, to believe this idea if you had been told you were a ‘natural’—rather than a cultural—subject your whole life. And especially if you lived in a world where everything from deodorant commercials to the supreme court believe people are natural subjects, with DNA that tells the truth of our bodies and higher consciousness that justifies us killing animals, etc.. basically what i’m saying: the category of person is a myth, but, like gender, a myth we are trapped in..*" p. 30

*"somehow, it’s like these cultural instructions which say ‘my perceptions make me into an individual’ are suddenly inactive and I can just perceive without being, or without being necessarily meaning that I am a human person with a name and socio-cultural laws and an assigned gender and a gendered history et cetera. This feeling of being not one single person, of being dialed in to just-experience, with no self, can also feel transcendent, though without any of the pomp or pretense that that word carries." p. 30*

It is the myth of personhood that traps the people seen / believed as 'less than' a person - queer people, non-white people, disabled people, poor people - that makes us feel dysphoric, that forces the label Dysphoric or Schizophrenic upon us. If this veil is lifted, though, when we feel the connection to the other, the disobjectifying, the dissubjectifying, everything loosens up and becomes Zero; that's when we feel Reality as is. Everything is Schizo, everything can Be, if we lose the labels to define us.

To see how full of possibility you are when the weight of dysphoria is lifted, when a world exists without it. How zeroed in you are, how full of potential. Those small moments when we're able to lift that veil, be it through disconnect from perception or drug-induced 'mis'perception, that is when we are free.
