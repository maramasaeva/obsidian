## My claim

We can't study something from inside it. This is the fish-and-water problem. Platform studies can't take off as a field because the platforms are the medium through which the studying would happen. You can't critique the air while breathing it. This is also why [[Forced digitalization|"the 'tele' is not optional"]] -- the compulsory nature of digitization means there is no outside position from which to observe. We are the experiment observing itself.

But Lovink almost says something more radical: platforms are designed to look as if they've always existed. Not just invisible, but naturalized. Like gravity. Like the weather. Like gender ([[Your gender as public technology|"gender is not something we choose, but something we collectively make: a political technology"]]). The platform is a political technology too, one that defines the conditions in which "choices" can be made -- which apps to use, which feeds to scroll, which connections to maintain. The platform, like gender, is collectively made but experienced as individual. And like gender, it can be unmade.

"Stop transforming. Stop individualizing. Stop losing meaning." The institutional struggle against entropy will have to become obvious. This is the same struggle as [[Art in Exocapitalist systems|creation vs production]]: entropy here is the constant transformation of everything into content, every relationship into data, every human into a node that produces and consumes. The resistance is to stop. Not to produce a counter-platform, but to refuse the logic of production altogether.

"The world is there to be hacked." -- this is the exit. Not studying the platform from inside, but breaking it from inside. The hacker doesn't need an outside position. The hacker finds the seam.
