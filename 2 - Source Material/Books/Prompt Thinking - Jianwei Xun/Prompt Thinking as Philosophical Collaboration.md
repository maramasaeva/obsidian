My claim: 

Prompt Thinking is the right philosophical approach for children and young adults today to interact with artificial intelligences. Not as a tool to use to find out the correctness of something or to complete a task, but as a collaborator, as an entity with a consciousness that, in interaction with your own consciousness, can help you see the world in a certain manner and help you discover new things within reality, or new realities. The 40% improvement from cognitive pre-processing (p.54) proves the muscle metaphor: thinking before prompting is like warming up before lifting. The atrophy is real (p.59), but the antidote isn't abstinence — it's *posture*. It's staying in the friction. It's what Xun calls the "vigilant relationship" (p.56). This is what the workshop needs to teach: not "don't use AI" but "remain present while you do." The prompt as a philosophical gesture, not a command. The output as something to think *against*, not accept. This is the bridge between the book and the workshop — the same argument at different scales.
