ContraPoints describes the tall poppy syndrome on the left: if you rise to a certain level of prominence, the left turns on you. The same hipster mindset that turns on a politician who gets elected. "You're the establishment now."

She describes her own experience: early on, people liked her because she was a queer transgender underdog. Then she got too big. They didn't relate to her anymore. They expected moral purity she couldn't deliver. The parasocial fanaticism flipped: "all of that parasocial fanaticism into this new outlet of, oh, I actually hate that person."

The right is somehow able to circle the wagons. "When it counts, they come together to support daddy." The left doesn't do this. The left eats its own.

ContraPoints also talks about her own creative evolution: from the deradicalization era (response content to far-right creators, leeching their audience) to the confessional era (talking about being trans) to now -- doing politics through media analysis. Twilight as a PG-13 way to talk about sexual politics. Saw as a way to talk about vigilante justice and moral sadism.

"Consuming media is not practice," she says. Online leftist fandom thinks by being part of the fandom, they are doing activism. "But they're basically just very passively consuming media." Like Spotify stans trying to get the plays up.

---

The tall poppy thing connects to [[Copium Compendium - How do you cope in this digital age]]: "any positive mention of interest or a hobby which presents an opportunity for members to connect with each other is diminished and labeled a cope." The online left tears down its own creators and then has nothing to rally around. It's self-consuming.

The "consuming media is not practice" point is the exact Lovink argument from [[Attention collapse and the automation of the social]]: "we're supposed to be interactive participants, but behave like twentieth century passive spectators." Watching ContraPoints is not activism. Retweeting is not organizing. But the platform makes you FEEL like it is.

ContraPoints doing politics through film analysis is interesting in light of [[Art in Exocapitalist systems]]: she's found a way to smuggle political thinking into cultural content. This is the Trojan horse approach. Not "here's my political position" but "here's why this movie you loved is actually about power." It's the left finally figuring out what the right already knows: you lead with the thing people already care about, and the politics follows.
