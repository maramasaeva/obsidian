ContraPoints makes the point that you basically can't deradicalize someone online. It doesn't work. Brandolini's law -- the energy needed to refute bullshit is an order of magnitude bigger than the energy to produce it. But more importantly: the medium itself is the problem. Online, people are performing for their audience. They can't change their mind in public because changing your mind looks like losing.

The deradicalization that does happen -- it happens in person. Through relationships, through IRL contact, through someone who actually knows you pulling you aside. Not through a well-argued video essay in their mentions.

Citarella adds to this: one of the three pillars of radicalization is social disconnection (the other two being economic stagnation and radicalizing media). ContraPoints agrees -- the formula is almost boringly straightforward. Lonely people + economic anxiety + algorithm-fed content = radicalization pipeline.

---

This connects directly to what Lovink is saying about [[Attention collapse and the automation of the social|the automation of the social]]. The platforms isolate us, and isolated people are vulnerable to radicalization. The solution -- "coming together and taking action" -- is exactly what the platform architecture prevents. The obstacle to deradicalization IS the medium.

Also connects to Jäger's [[Hyperpolitics]]: "politics without infrastructure. engagement without commitment. everyone has opinions, nobody has a party card." The organizational layer that used to catch people -- unions, churches, parties, clubs -- is gone. There's nothing to belong to that isn't online, and online belonging is parasocial, not real. You don't get deradicalized by a comment section. You get deradicalized by a friend.

And the Preciado point from [[Fighting offline battles in online spaces - DIGITAL COUP]]: "social movements were locked down, removed from the physical spheres." If even progressive movements can only exist online now, how do we do the IRL work that actually changes minds?
