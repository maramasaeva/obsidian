ContraPoints connects Freud's idea of guilt as repressed aggression turned inward to the Saw movies. Morality has a sadistic core. When you internalize the law -- the punishing father, the superego -- it becomes a tyrannical voice inside your head. Jigsaw is a cartoon version of that. Punishing people's sins through ironic torture.

She brings up anorexia as an example of the same logic: some forms of anorexia involve a "sadistic superego" where you're disciplining yourself for an aesthetic sin. Citarella adds bodybuilding -- same mechanism, same food restriction, same physical discipline. ContraPoints agrees: "moral bodybuilding is exactly the same."

So anorexia, bodybuilding, self-harm, Jigsaw's traps -- all versions of turning aggression inward as punishment, with the superego as torturer. The discipline isn't health. It's self-directed sadism dressed up as virtue.

---

There's something else here too. The obsession with thinness, with the "perfect" body -- this maps onto the aesthetics/reality split from [[Right-wing aesthetics vs left-wing reality]] in a way that's uncomfortable.

Right-wing men who are obsessed with aesthetics tend to be obsessed with women who look a certain way -- thin, controlled, disciplined. The anorexic body as an aesthetic ideal. It's not about health and it's not about the woman. It's about the image. The body as proof of discipline, of willpower, of control over nature. The same logic as their art: beautiful, ordered, nothing out of place.

And then there's a specific subset of white gay men who lean right or right-adjacent, who are obsessed with their own bodies in a similar way. The gym, the cutting, the aesthetics of the male form. Not all gay men obviously, and being obsessed with aesthetics doesn't make you right-wing. But there's a particular intersection -- white, male, gay, affluent -- where the obsession with bodily perfection starts to rhyme with right-wing aesthetics. The body becomes something to be sculpted, optimized, perfected. Not lived in. Performed.

The difference feels like this: when someone on the left engages with their body, it tends to be about becoming, about feeling at home in yourself, about transformation as liberation. When the aesthetic-right does it -- whether straight men demanding thin women or a certain type of gay man sculpting himself -- it's about control. About imposing order on flesh. The body as material to be disciplined, not inhabited. And that discipline has the same sadistic superego energy ContraPoints is describing. You punish the body for not being perfect. The punishment IS the point. The suffering proves the devotion.

This is looksmaxing taken to its logical extreme. It's not self-improvement. It's self-punishment rebranded as self-improvement. And the people most susceptible to it are the ones most captured by the aesthetic worldview -- where how you look IS who you are, and any imperfection is a moral failing.
