Work has literally no value anymore in the current state of exocapitalism. Not metaphorically. Literally. The system doesn't need your labor to generate capital. It generates capital through volatility, through time, through financial instruments that have nothing to do with what you make or do. Your work is worthless to the machine.

And I think this connects to why everything feels meaningless. It's not just that we've lost hope in the future, or that everything's become possible so nothing feels necessary anymore. It's that the thing we used to point to as proof of our worth -- our work, our labor, what we make and do -- has had its value hollowed out. Not by us. By the system floating above us.

This is what I was already feeling with music. Every time I found meaning in a song, the novelty disappeared ([[Nomos of the network|"the digitalization of music has not opened up any new vistas but instead contributed to the fact that most music is infinitely uniform and boring"]]). And it's what happened in the psych ward when all my hobbies became "coping mechanisms" ([[Copium Compendium - How do you cope in this digital age|"all of my interests became a way to cope with stress or trauma. And because of that, those things lost their original meaning"]]). The moment you attach an external purpose to the thing -- whether that's a wage, a coping strategy, a follower count, a Spotify stream -- the thing itself dies. "Perfection is the highest stage of depression" ([[Nomos of the network|p. 144]]).

So if the external value of work is gone -- if the system genuinely doesn't care about what you produce -- then maybe what we need to do is move what we see as valuable. Not look for a new way to attach a price to labor. Accept that the price is gone. And instead find value in the process itself. The doing of it. What it gives back to you and to the person next to you. Not the output, not the product, not the number. The act.

This is the incalculable again ([[AGI's natural enemy]]). The system can eat the product. It can eat the output. It can quantify the hours, the streams, the engagement. But it can't eat what making something does to you while you're making it. It can't eat the feeling of your hands doing a thing. It can't eat the conversation that happens while you work next to someone. That's incalculable. That's what's left.

And maybe that reframes the slavery question from the podcast ([[The death of labor and the left's blind spot]]). If labor has no calculable value anymore, then yes, in the system's terms, any labor is slavery -- work for no pay, because the pay can't compete. But if you shift what "value" means -- from the calculable (wages, price, output) to the incalculable (meaning, connection, the act itself) -- then it's not slavery. It's something else. It's work that the system can't touch because it was never for the system in the first place.

I don't know if this holds. It might be naive. It sounds a bit like "just find meaning in your work" which is the kind of thing that ignores the fact that people need to eat. But I think the point is more structural: the system has abandoned us, so we need to build something in the space it left behind. And that something can't be built with the system's tools or the system's language. It has to be built with whatever is incalculable. Whatever dies when you try to turn it into data.

## Art as work: the costume that lets the incalculable survive

Something I noticed in my own practice: the only way I could get myself to actually make art was to start treating it as work. Not as a hobby, not as something I do in my free time, not as something I enjoy. Work. And only then did it have meaning. Only then could I force myself to sit down, do it, and actually get something back from it.

Which says everything about where we are. In this society, work is the only category that's recognized as legitimate. "I like doing this" is not enough. "This is my work" is. The system has so completely colonized what counts as valuable that even the person making art can't give themselves permission unless they dress it up in the system's language. The incalculable wearing the costume of the calculable so it can survive.

But the realization itself helped. Because once I called it work, the discipline kicked in. I showed up. I did the thing. And the doing of the thing gave me something real. So maybe the discipline of treating it as work isn't just a trick. Maybe discipline is what creates the conditions for the incalculable to happen. You can't just wait for it. You have to sit down. And sitting down requires a reason that feels real enough. And in this society, "it's my work" is the only reason that passes.

There's something here about discipline having two faces. Too much discipline broke me -- the same discipline that makes you show up to make art is the discipline that makes you restrict food, that makes you punish your body for not being perfect ([[The sadistic superego and aesthetic discipline]]). The superego as torturer. Discipline turned inward as self-punishment. But discipline turned outward, toward a practice, toward something you're building -- that's different. Or maybe it's not different at all and the line between the two is thinner than I want it to be. I'm still thinking about this.

The sad part is that "I want to" should be enough reason to make something. The fact that it isn't says everything about exocapitalism. But the realization itself -- that I need to call it work, and that's okay, and it actually works -- that has helped me more than any theory about why it shouldn't be necessary.
