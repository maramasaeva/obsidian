## Key concepts from the podcast

**Lift**: the sensation of being lifted away from. As companies transition from hardware (physical goods) to software (financial services, loyalty points, abstract partnerships), they stop being interested in selling things to people. They're interested in hitting the "generate capital forever" button. And so you, the person walking through the world, are left behind. Nothing is really about you anymore. Lift is the affect of that abandonment.

**Scale is violence**: the shift in scale can be a kind of violence. The same thing at a different scale generates wildly different externalities. A car can only be built to a certain size before it stops being a car. An interaction can only be scaled so far before it stops being an interaction. The inability to understand scale is at the root of so much pain and confusion.

**Capitalism is not sociogenic**: exocapitalism argues capitalism was not generated by humans. It has nothing to do with human causality. It was always there as a potentiality, a virus, something that works through algorithmic means independent of us. It's not an enemy -- it's an indifferent meta process. A cancer that doesn't care about us.

**Value = inefficiency**: not labor. Marx situated capitalism in the transition from feudal to industrial Europe, rooted in labor dynamics. But contemporary financial capitalism doesn't work that way. Value is created from nothing -- taking a thing (silver, a token, a futures contract) and letting time pass, letting volatility do its work. Labor isn't value-add; it's a volatility engine injecting randomness into an underlying relationship of value. Buy, hold, sell. The value is in the hold.

**The functional death of labor**: labor operates at the scales most proximal to our immediate needs. Those scales categorically don't matter to the stochastic hedging and betting that generates capital today. Unionizing at Starbucks is reasonable, but will it change Starbucks, whose business model is increasingly an IP layer and essentially a bank? The highest value production happens where companies aren't selling things to people at all.

**Code is sovereign**: there is no human avatar for responsibility. The Always Sunny in Philadelphia example -- the guy trying to find who owns the driverless car company, spiraling through customer service agents who are all following decision trees. The only expression of agency he can find is drilling down to a human and shooting them. Luigi mode. But there is no single human dependency that owns any of this.

**AI interaction is a scale problem**: when you open ChatGPT, you're pulling up a dead checkpoint sharded across servers. You type a paragraph and it finds the next statistically likely token. That's not interaction. The only way you can touch OpenAI is through the activity of thousands of people bubbling up into product decisions that asynchronously work their way through feedback layers. But it can touch you.
