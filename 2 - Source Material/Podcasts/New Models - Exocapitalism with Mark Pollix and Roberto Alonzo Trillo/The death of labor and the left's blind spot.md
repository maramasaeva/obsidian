## What I said

Something I realized while listening: there was this thing I said about the left only reacting to problems based in reality and the right reacting to problems based in aesthetics. And what I find interesting is the relevance of labor.

Labor is obviously seen as very important especially by the left. It's important for the right too because labor used to be the means in which something could be produced and sold physically. However, in today's age where capital is moving from hardware to software, physical labor has become increasingly less important and increasingly less generating of new value.

So that means the typical ways in which the left tries to regain its power -- especially the labor force, by unionizing, organizing, strikes, protests -- it won't eventually do much. Because those are actions at the level of the lowest possible value production. The highest value production is in these areas where companies aren't really selling things face-to-face, physically making things, but more in the virtual space where value is basically being generated by its movement. If value moves, that's what generates something.

---

This is a hard thing to sit with. The left's entire playbook -- organize, unionize, strike, protest -- operates at the scale where value used to be created. But value has moved. It's not in the factory anymore. It's not even in the service layer. It's in the abstract financial layer where Starbucks is essentially a bank and airlines make money from fuel hedging contracts, not from flying people places. Mark says in the podcast: labor is powerless because it operates at the scales most proximal to our immediate needs, and those scales categorically don't matter to the stochastic hedging and betting that generates capital today.

This connects to [[Debating techno-feudalism]]: "profit has been replaced by rent." If you're unionizing at the retail layer but the actual capital generation happens three layers of abstraction above you, you're fighting the wrong battle at the wrong scale. It's not that organizing doesn't matter -- Mark explicitly says unionizing at Starbucks is reasonable -- but it's not going to change the thing you think you're changing.

Also connects to what Lovink says in [[Copium Compendium - How do you cope in this digital age]]: "the spirit of capital may be dead, but so is the will to resist and overthrow the regime." Maybe the will to resist isn't dead. Maybe the tools to resist are dead. The left still has the will. It just doesn't have mechanisms that operate at the scale where capital actually lives now.

And the [[Right-wing aesthetics vs left-wing reality]] argument gets more complicated here. The left focuses on reality -- real labor, real wages, real conditions. But if reality has shifted, if the real value creation has floated up into abstract financial space, then the left's attachment to material reality is also a kind of blindness. Not because material conditions don't matter (they do, people are suffering), but because the power to change those conditions no longer lives at that scale.
