The traditional art world dissolved in the 2010s. Not because art stopped being made but because the infrastructure that held it together — shared references, linear media, a common public sphere, institutional gatekeeping that at least pretended to be coherent — fell apart. The most interesting artists by the 2020s didn't call themselves artists. They were online. They had no interest in galleries.

So Remilia didn't just make a network. It made its own art world. Its own economy (crypto), its own criticism (Remilia Quarterly), its own aesthetic language (Milady posting), its own system of value (cultural capital captured through NFTs under viral public license). It doesn't need validation from the old art world because that art world is a shadow of itself.

And maybe this is the future: not one art world but many, each network-specific. Each with its own protocol of value and communication and status. Art worlds as networks. Networks as art worlds.

This connects to the exocapitalism "lift" concept — the traditional art world experienced lift. It got left behind by where value actually moved to. The institutions are still there, still doing shows, still writing press releases, but the energy and the money and the interesting people are somewhere else. Same way Starbucks is essentially a bank and airlines make money from fuel hedging — art institutions are essentially bureaucracies that happen to show art.

And it connects to the death of the common public sphere. Critical art only worked when everyone saw it. A critical gesture on a fragmented timeline reaches nobody. So you need a different kind of art — art that operates at the protocol level, that creates its own conditions of visibility instead of relying on a shared stage that doesn't exist anymore.

The question I have is whether this is actually new or whether art has always worked this way and the "art world" was just a temporary historical arrangement. Like, were there always many art worlds and we just had a period where one of them dominated?
