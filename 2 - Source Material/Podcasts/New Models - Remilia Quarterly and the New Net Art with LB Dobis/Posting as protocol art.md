There's something in the Remilia model that flips the whole "art in exocapitalist systems" problem. If art has degenerated into advertising and content is cheap and the system doesn't need your labor, then what Remilia did is stop making content and start making protocol. The posting formulas — set a template, write from the tip of your mind, scale through thousands — that's not content creation. It's creating a wave that operates at the algorithmic level. They're not making art objects. They're making conditions under which art happens.

And this connects to something weird about authenticity. The millennial instinct is: this can't be real art because it's formulaic, it's directed, it's not individual expression. But Remilia's whole point is that individual expression on social media was always an illusion. You were always directed by the algorithm. The difference is Remilia admits it and works with it. The "tip of your mind" trance-state posting is arguably more authentic than deliberating for hours over a tweet, because at least you're not pretending the platform isn't shaping you.

This is the hacker mode vs the critical mode. Millennials critique the system — they make self-aware art that performs its knowledge of its own commodification. But critique only works when there's a common public sphere to see it. There isn't one anymore. There's just the timeline. So critique is a parlor game. Hacking actually does something.

But I'm not sure I buy it completely. There's something about the Milady thing that feels like it's still operating in the system's terms — attention, velocity, cultural capital captured as financial value through NFTs. Like, is the VPL really different from the system or is it just the system wearing a cooler outfit? The value is still calculated — it's just measured in cultural capital instead of labor. Is that the incalculable? Or is it just a different kind of calculable?

I keep coming back to the fact that the best 4chan posters became the best algorithmic posters. The best copywriters of our generation are 4chan posters. That's funny and also kind of terrifying. The skills transfer perfectly because both environments are about capturing attention at speed. The medium changed but the game didn't.

## Remilia and the labor theory of value

There's a direct line between this and the exocapitalism episode ([[New Models - Exocapitalism with Mark Pollix and Roberto Alonzo Trillo/notes|NM - Exocapitalism]]). LB Dobis says older artists approach NFTs thinking "I put work into this, so it's valuable" — and Remilia finds that totally absurd. That's a Marxist labor theory of value: labor = value. But Mark Pollix already said that's dead. Value isn't labor, it's inefficiency. Buy, hold, sell. The value is in the hold, in volatility, in time passing. Remilia instinctively gets this. They don't sell labor. They sell cultural capital, identity, velocity of a sign on the timeline. The NFT isn't valuable because someone worked hard on it. It's valuable because it captures the energy of a subculture moving at speed.

So Remilia actually *understands* exocapitalism and acts accordingly, even without using the word. They're not fighting the system or critiquing it. They're building at the scale where value actually lives now. The protocol level, not the labor level. This is what the left can't do ([[The death of labor and the left's blind spot]]) — operate at the scale where capital actually moves.

But then the [[The process as the only value left|process-as-value]] argument complicates this. Because that note says the *incalculable* part of work — what making something does to you while you make it — is the thing the system can't eat. And Remilia is doing the opposite: they've made the output maximally calculable (NFT price, cultural capital, attention metrics) and the process is just a formula. Post from the tip of your mind, follow the template, scale through thousands.

So the question is: is there anything incalculable left in Milady posting? Or has Remilia solved exocapitalism by fully accepting the calculable and abandoning the incalculable entirely? And if so, is that a victory or a surrender? Because the trance-state posting, the stream consciousness writing — maybe that IS incalculable. Maybe what happens to you in the trance is the part that can't be extracted. The formula is calculable. What it does to the person following the formula might not be. Like how discipline creates conditions for the incalculable to happen ([[The process as the only value left|"discipline is what creates the conditions for the incalculable to happen"]]).

Or maybe Remilia just proves that the incalculable doesn't matter. That you can build an entire art world without it. And the fact that it works — that it's the most interesting thing happening in art right now — is the uncomfortable part.
