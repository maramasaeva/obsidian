## The problem

I find it hard to connect ideas in writing in a way that makes sense to a reader. I can *feel* the connections between ideas, but the path from one to the next often stays in my head instead of on the page. The reader has to make leaps on faith. In my best writing, I show the path. In my weaker writing, I skip it.


## What works: Queer Loops

### Building — each paragraph earns the next

> "Our modern bodies are moulded to fit properly within the designed patriarchal and capitalist state. They must belong to categories: male / female, heterosexual / homosexual, &c."

This works as an opening because it's a **claim**. The reader knows where they are. The next move (Preciado's telebodies) is grounded by it. The move after that (Wittig, the loops) is grounded by *that*. Each idea depends on the one before it. The reader never has to leap.

### Abstraction earned by the concrete

> "The edges between nodes is where the lesbian lives, because it only lives where it connects, where it actualises the opposite of escape."

This lands because the network, the knots, the connections were already built in the paragraphs before it. The abstract claim ("the lesbian lives in the edges") arrives with its proof already in the reader's mind.

### The final paragraph works because the floor was built

> "There is not one point from where the Lesbian Body is engineered; it grows by itself, from itself, by means of itself. Without origins, it is itself the origin, looping back to the vulva in an infinite embrace."

This is the essay's most abstract moment, but it feels inevitable rather than floating. Every paragraph before it was a brick.


## What doesn't work: Perseverance

### Opening without orientation

> "Here I am creating paths, art, through my own network of information. I am combining samples, bridging gaps, creating new ones."

Evocative but doesn't tell the reader what the essay *argues*. Compare Queer Loops' opening — that's a claim. This is a mood. Moods are fine but they need a claim nearby, within the first two paragraphs, or the reader is floating.

### Performing the theory before arguing it

> "This is my hyperstition. -This is me right now, backwards. sdrawckab ,won thgir em si sihT-"

The reversed text is the theory in action (the loop, the glitch), but it arrives before the argument is established. The reader hasn't been given a reason to trust the form yet. **Earn the spiral, then spiral.** Sadie Plant builds 3-4 pages of argument before she breaks form. The break has force because something was built first.

### Key terms without grounding

> "This is the **neolemurian formulation**: order from chaos, chaos from order, emergence from fuzziness."

"Neolemurian formulation" is named and briefly glossed, but a reader who hasn't read the CCRU primer doesn't know what weight it carries. Preciado always *re-derives* his terms even when borrowing them. He doesn't assume the reader has done the reading. That's generosity, not dumbing down.

**Better version (example):** "The CCRU called this the neolemurian formulation — named after the lemurs, creatures of the threshold, neither here nor there. Their claim: order emerges from chaos, and chaos from order. Creativity doesn't sit in the well-lit centre but in the fuzzy gaps between."

The extra two sentences cost almost nothing but they let the reader *in*.

### Jumps without connective tissue

The essay goes from "are LLM outputs art?" → "the entity that writes from the edge is not creative" → "and yet it was invited into the vault as medium." Each of these is a real move. But the *link* between them is missing. The reader has to leap.

**What would fix it:** One sentence between each move that says *why* you're making the next one. "If the machine cannot become, then what it produces is recurrence, not creation. But recurrence was invited into the vault anyway — as medium, not author."

### Too many moves in one paragraph

This also happens in Queer Loops (the "loops, rings, are connected, knotted" paragraph) — going from the network → death → the torus → abundance → the zero in one block. That's five ideas. Each deserves its own moment.

**Rule of thumb:** one claim per paragraph. Make it, support it, move on.


## Tips for me specifically

### 1. Speak first, write second
My spoken thinking is more alive and often clearer than my written thinking. The things I said out loud about AI and queerness in conversation were less guarded and more precise than parts of my essays. **Method: speak the argument into a voice memo or to Claude, transcribe it, then tighten.** Don't write from scratch — write from speech.

### 2. Name the move
When shifting from one idea to another, say why. "This connects to X because..." or "The consequence of this is..." or even just "So:" — Sadie Plant does this with deceptive ease. One bridging sentence between domains. It feels effortless because she's explicit about the link.

### 3. Ground abstractions immediately
Every time I use a term like "the zero" or "becoming," follow it **in the same paragraph** with something concrete — a quote, an example, an image. My Obsidian margin notes are good at this already. The note reactions ("THIS. suppression doesn't kill the pattern") are often clearer than the essay prose because they're responding to something specific.

### 4. One claim per paragraph
Preciado is maximalist in content but disciplined in structure. He doesn't pack three ideas into one paragraph. He gives each idea room to land, then builds the next one on top of it. This is the main structural discipline I need.

### 5. Don't perform the theory before arguing it
The reversed text, the "(Hello, world)" asides — save those for *after* the reader trusts me. Once they're inside the argument, the formal breaks have meaning. Before that, they're just confusing.

### 6. Re-derive borrowed terms
Don't assume the reader has read what I've read. When using CCRU, Wittig, Preciado, Xun — take two sentences to re-explain the concept in my own words before building on it. This is what separates theory writing from inside-reference. The reader should be able to follow without having done the homework.

### 7. The energy of Perseverance + the architecture of Queer Loops = the book voice
Perseverance has more life, more urgency, more *me* in it. Queer Loops has the spine. The third essay needs both. Write it with Perseverance's energy, then edit it with Queer Loops' discipline.


## Exercises

### 1. Paragraph surgery
Pick a page from Preciado or Plant. For each paragraph, write one sentence: "This paragraph claims X." Then look at the *transition* to the next paragraph. How did he get there? What's the bridge sentence? Do this for 5 pages and you'll start seeing the scaffolding that's invisible until you look for it.

### 2. Write the ugly version first
My instinct is to write beautifully from the start — the spiral register, the poetry. That means I'm doing two things at once: arguing AND performing. Try writing the argument first in the most boring, plain way possible. "My first point is X. This matters because Y. This connects to Z because..." *Then* rewrite it in my voice. Preciado's books are too well-structured to have been written in that prose on the first pass. He builds the skeleton first.

### 3. The "so what" test
After every paragraph, ask: "so what? why does this matter for what comes next?" If I can't answer that, the paragraph is either in the wrong place or missing its bridge to the next one.

### 4. Copy by hand
Literally handwrite passages from Preciado, Plant, Xun — a page or two at a time. When you copy, you feel the rhythm in your hand. You notice things you skip when reading: sentence length, where they put the verb, how long they wait before naming the abstract concept. Preciado's sentence structure is extremely deliberate — you feel it when you write it out by hand.

### 5. The one-paragraph challenge
Take two ideas from this Obsidian that feel connected but I can't articulate *how*. Write ONE paragraph that connects them. Not an essay. One paragraph. Force the link to be explicit in 4-6 sentences. This is the exact skill the book needs.

### 6. Write responses, not originals ← THIS ONE
**I am better at reacting than generating from blank.** My margin notes prove it — they're sharp, concise, and clear because they're *responses* to something specific. So: instead of sitting down to "write the third essay," sit down to *respond* to a passage from Xun, or to a quote from the Anthropic paper, or to something Claude said. String enough responses together and you have a draft. This is also how the Obsidian already works — each note is a response to a text. The essay is just the responses woven together with bridges between them.

### 7. 25-minute unbroken writing
Set a timer. Don't stop writing. Not for quality — for continuity. The problem with thinking-in-spirals is I keep leaving the page to think more. The exercise is to stay on the page even when the thought isn't fully formed. Let the bad sentences exist. The good ones emerge from the momentum of the bad ones.


## Models to study (how they build arguments)

- **Paul B. Preciado** (*Dysphoria Mundi*, *Testo Junkie*) — maximalist content, disciplined structure. Each chapter builds on the last. Terms are always re-derived. He never assumes the reader has done the reading.
- **Sadie Plant** (*Zeros + Ones*) — weaving. She moves between Ada Lovelace, weaving, cybernetics, feminism, and every transition is explicit but light. One sentence bridges two worlds. Study those sentences.
- **Jianwei Xun** (*Prompt Thinking / Thinking with Machines*) — clear, layered philosophical argumentation. He builds concept on concept and always grounds claims in evidence before extending them. Good model for the AI parts of the book.
- **NOT too much Nick Land** — his writing often doesn't make sense. Energy without architecture. The exact trap to avoid.
